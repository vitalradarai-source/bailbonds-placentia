Placentia-Specific Facts You Need

VC §40508 — failure to appear on a traffic citation — is the trap that catches drivers years after they thought a ticket was forgotten. The statute makes the FTA itself a misdemeanor punishable by up to 6 months in jail and a $1,000 fine, separate from whatever the original traffic violation was. North County drivers often discover the warrant during a routine traffic stop years later.

The OC bench-warrant bail schedule typically sets VC §40508 bail at $5,000 for a single FTA, $7,500 for two, $10,000 for three or more. When the original ticket was for a more serious VC violation (DUI, reckless driving, hit-and-run), bail can be substantially higher. Placentia PD handles bench-warrant arrests at 401 E. Chapman Ave, with transport to the Anaheim Detention Facility within 6-12 hours.

Two North County practical notes. First, the underlying traffic case isn't gone — once the warrant is cleared, the original ticket must still be resolved at the Fullerton Court (which serves Placentia). Trying to "make the warrant go away" without addressing the underlying VC charge typically results in a second warrant within months. Second, traffic warrants in Placentia are often eligible for a "walk-in" appearance — defense counsel can sometimes arrange a court appearance that clears the warrant without bail being posted. The bondsman-first approach makes sense when (a) the defendant is already in custody, (b) work or family commitments require immediate release, or (c) the warrant amount is small enough that bond cost ($500-$1,000 premium) is worth the time savings.

The recall-the-warrant motion under VC §40509 is the defense move that often resolves these cases without a bond at all. The motion asks the court to vacate the bench warrant and reinstate the original case on the calendar. If granted, the defendant appears voluntarily on the new date and the FTA charge is typically dismissed as part of the resolution. Coordinating with defense counsel before posting bail can sometimes save the entire bond cost.

What VC §40508 Actually Says

California Vehicle Code §40508 covers: Failure to appear on traffic citation — separate misdemeanor with own bail amount. Understanding the statute matters because the bail schedule, no-bail-hold rules, and protective-order rules all flow from how the prosecutor charges the case.

Three points families typically miss in VC §40508 cases: the charging decision is not final at booking — the arresting agency's charge can change at the DA's filing review (within 48 hours under PC §825). Bail schedule amounts are not ceilings — a judge at arraignment can raise bail above the schedule based on flight risk or danger, or lower it on defense motion under In re Humphrey (2021). Protective orders can attach automatically — under PC §136.2, a Criminal Protective Order can issue at arraignment with a 100-yard stay-away clause that may bar the defendant from returning home.

Bail Cost and the 10% Premium

The bail amount and the cost to post a bond are two different numbers. The bail amount is the dollar figure the court requires to guarantee appearance — set per the North Orange County bail schedule and subject to judicial discretion at arraignment.

The cost to a family is the 10% statutory premium under California Insurance Code §1800.4. This premium is a fee for the surety service, not money lent to the customer — it is non-refundable regardless of case outcome (acquittal, dismissal, conviction, plea). The 10% rate is set by California Department of Insurance regulation; no licensed bondsman can charge a different rate. A bondsman quoting 5% or 8% is either operating outside the law or planning to add fees later.

Mistakes to Avoid in the First 24 Hours

  1. Don't wait for arraignment to act. Bail can be posted as soon as booking is complete — typically within 2-6 hours of arrest.
  2. Don't pay cash bail without checking with a bondsman first. Cash bail ties up the full bail amount until case resolution.
  3. Don't sign indemnitor paperwork you haven't read. The indemnitor agreement is a binding contract.
  4. Don't coach the defendant on what to say at booking. Phone calls from jail are recorded.
  5. Don't violate a protective order, even verbally. If a PC §136.2 CPO issues at arraignment, contact through any means can result in a new charge under PC §166.
